Chapter 1: Introduction to Covalent Bonds
Covalent bonds occur when atoms share electrons to achieve stable electron configurations. Unlike ionic bonds, which involve electron transfer, covalent bonds result from the simultaneous sharing of electrons. This sharing leads to the formation of molecules, where atoms are held together by strong covalent bonds. Key differences between covalent and ionic bonds include electron sharing versus electron transfer.
Chapter 2: How Covalent Bonds Work
Covalent bonds are based on the principle of electron sharing, allowing atoms to achieve full outer electron shells, typically containing eight electrons (following the octet rule). Valence electrons, the outermost electrons, participate in bonding. This chapter explores how atoms share electrons to form covalent bonds, enabling a stable electron configuration.
Chapter 3: Forces in Covalent Bonds
Covalent bonds involve the electrostatic attraction between atomic nuclei and shared electrons. This force holds atoms together within molecules. Bond strength depends on factors like the number of shared electrons and interatomic distances. Understanding these forces is essential for comprehending covalent compound properties.
Chapter 4: Drawing Covalent Bonds
Lewis structures offer a graphical representation of covalent bonds, depicting atom arrangements and shared electrons. Drawing Lewis structures involves assigning valence electrons, forming bonds (lines), and adhering to the octet rule. This skill aids in understanding molecular structure and predicting chemical reactions.

Displayed formulas provide detailed notations for covalent compounds, illustrating atom connectivity and bond arrangements. Chapter 6: Three-Dimensional Structure of Covalent Compounds
Covalent compounds exist in three-dimensional space, adopting various shapes. Understanding spatial arrangements and bond angles is crucial for predicting molecular properties. Common molecular geometries include linear, trigonal planar, and tetrahedral shapes, each determined by atom arrangement and bond angles.
Chapter 7: Different Covalent Bonding Structures
Covalent bonds come in various forms, including single, double, and triple bonds. These result from varying degrees of electron sharing. Such structural diversity significantly influences molecular properties and reactivity, playing a vital role in chemical reactions.
Chapter 8: Covalent Bonding Example - Water (H2O)
Water (H2O) serves as a prime example of covalent bonding. Oxygen and hydrogen atoms share electrons to create this molecule. This electron sharing results in the unique properties of water, including its high polarity, which leads to hydrogen bonding and various essential features for life.
Chapter 9: Covalent Bonding Example - Methane (CH4)
Methane (CH4) exemplifies covalent bonding in hydrocarbons. Carbon and hydrogen atoms share electrons, forming covalent bonds. The molecule takes on a tetrahedral shape due to electron distribution. Methane is a critical component of natural gas and plays a role in various industrial processes.
Chapter 10: Covalent Bonding Example - Ethylene (C2H4)
Ethylene (C2H4) showcases covalent bonding with a double bond between carbon atoms. These carbon atoms share two pairs of electrons, resulting in a planar molecular geometry. Ethylene is utilized in the chemical industry and plays a role in plant biology, influencing fruit ripening and growth regulation.
