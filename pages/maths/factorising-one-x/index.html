Factorising Quadratics with a Coefficient of One
When you have a quadratic equation in the form x^2 + bx + c, where the coefficient of x^2 is 1, you can factorise it to find the expressions that, when multiplied, give you the quadratic expression. Factorisation allows you to rewrite the quadratic in the form (x + ...)(x + ...), which reveals its roots.
Trial and Error Method
The trial and error method is a straightforward approach to factorising. To use this method, you need to find two numbers that multiply to the constant term (c) and add up to the coefficient of the middle term (b). These numbers will help you rewrite the quadratic expression as (x + ...)(x + ...).
For example, consider the quadratic expression x^2 + 5x + 6. You can identify the numbers 2 and 3 that meet the criteria. This allows you to rewrite the expression as (x + 2)(x + 3).
Examples
Let's explore the trial and error method with a few examples. Consider the expression x^2 + 7x + 12. Using this method, you can find that 3 and 4 are suitable numbers. This leads to the factorisation (x + 3)(x + 4).
Now, take the expression x^2 - 6x - 16. By identifying the numbers 2 and 8, you can rewrite it as (x - 8)(x + 2).
Conclusion
Factorising quadratics with a coefficient of one using the trial and error method is a fundamental skill in algebra. It helps you express the quadratic in the form (x + ...)(x + ...), revealing its roots. Understanding this technique is crucial for solving a wide range of mathematical problems.
